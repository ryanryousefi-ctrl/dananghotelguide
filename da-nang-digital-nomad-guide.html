Digital Nomad Da Nang

Da Nang has become one of Southeast Asia's most established remote-work destinations — fast internet, a serious café culture, a beach 10 minutes from most co-working spaces, and a cost of living that makes a 3-month stay cheaper than a single month in most Western cities.

Why Da Nang

Da Nang for Remote Workers

An Thuong neighbourhood cafés — most run fibre-speed WiFi, good espresso, and don't mind a 4-hour working session

Da Nang ranked in the top 20 of Nomad List's global remote-work index for three consecutive years between 2021 and 2024, and the infrastructure that drove that ranking — fast internet, a functional co-working ecosystem, and a high café-to-resident ratio — has only improved since. The city is not Bangkok or Bali in terms of nomad density, which is arguably its main advantage: fewer digital nomads means rents that reflect local rather than expat demand, and café tables that are available on a Tuesday morning.

The core appeal: a beach and a serious working infrastructure in the same city, with a cost of living that allows a six-month stay for what a single month costs in London, Sydney, or New York. The main friction point — Vietnam's visa regime — improved significantly in 2023 with the introduction of the 90-day e-visa and the five-year multiple-entry visa for eligible passport holders. See the visa section for current options.

The best working season is February through August — the dry season, when reliable weather, stable electricity (no typhoon disruption), and the best café ambience coincide. September through November brings the monsoon, which affects outdoor lifestyle significantly but has minimal impact on indoor work.

Where to Base Yourself

Best Neighbourhoods

Da Nang's three remote-work-friendly neighbourhoods offer genuinely different environments — the beach-adjacent tourist strip, the authentic Vietnamese city centre, and a quieter residential zone close to local markets.

An Thuong Village sits one block from My Khe Beach — the densest café and restaurant zone in Da Nang

An Thuong Village / My Khe Beach — best for first stays. The An Thuong neighbourhood (bounded by An Thượng 1–4 streets, one block from the beach) has the highest concentration of international-facing cafés, restaurants, and co-working spaces in Da Nang. Most offer stable WiFi, Western coffee, and an environment that works for extended laptop sessions. Rent for a studio apartment with beach view: $350–550/month. Rent without sea view but a 2-minute walk: $250–350/month. Grocery access: two full supermarkets (Bách Hoá Xanh and a Co.opmart) within 10 minutes' walk. The environment is cosmopolitan and tourist-adjacent — not ideal if you want full cultural immersion, but very practical for a first month.

Hải Châu District — best for culture and cost. Da Nang's central urban district, bounded by the Han River to the east and the airport road to the west. Rent is 25–40% lower than An Thuong for comparable apartments. The café culture is strong but more local in character — Vietnamese-style coffee shops with drip cà phê and small tables rather than Western espresso bars. The morning market and Hàn Market are both here, providing the best daily food shopping in the city. Internet infrastructure is equivalent to An Thuong. The trade-off: 10–15 minutes further from the beach. Recommended for stays of one month or more where integration into local life is part of the goal.

Son Tra Peninsula — quieter, greener, more expensive. Son Tra has the most scenic living environment in Da Nang: forested hillside, several quiet beaches accessible by motorbike, and a significantly lower tourist density than My Khe. Suitable for remote workers who prioritise outdoor access over café density. Co-working options are limited — most Son Tra residents work from home or make the 20-minute journey to An Thuong or Hải Châu for co-working. Rent is higher than Hải Châu (comparable to An Thuong) due to the resort hotel premium in the area. Long-term apartment availability is limited; the Airbnb market is more active here than the traditional rental market.

🏠 Finding accommodation

For stays under 30 days, Airbnb and Booking.com give the widest options. For 1–3 month stays, the Facebook group "Da Nang Expats" and the local agency listings on batdongsan.com.vn have direct landlord rentals at significantly lower rates. Most landlords in the An Thuong area are familiar with short-term foreigner leases — a 3-month contract is standard.

Co-Working Spaces

Co-Working Spaces

Da Nang has a growing co-working sector — smaller and less polished than Ho Chi Minh City or Hanoi, but functional, affordable, and with the community infrastructure that long-term nomads value.

Da Nang co-working spaces are concentrated in An Thuong and the Han River district — hot desks from $4–6/day, monthly memberships from $80

Toong Da Nang. The local branch of Toong — Vietnam's largest co-working chain — sits on Nguyễn Văn Linh near the Han River bridge. Hot desk: $5/day, $80/month. Private desk: $130–160/month. Facilities: high-speed fibre, printing, meeting rooms, phone booths, and a rooftop terrace. English-speaking front desk. The most professionally managed co-working in the city.

Beat Space Da Nang. A smaller, design-focused space in the An Thuong area with a strong community of local creative and tech freelancers. Day pass: $6. Monthly: $90. More communal than Toong — better for those who want to meet local professionals. Regular evening events and skill-share sessions. WiFi is consistently fast (tested at 150–200 Mbps on multiple visits).

The Nest. A hybrid café-co-working space in Hải Châu popular with Vietnamese startup founders and English-teaching freelancers. Day pass: $4 (includes one coffee). No monthly option. Open 7am–10pm. The ground floor café atmosphere can be noisy in the mornings — the upstairs dedicated working area is quieter.

Hotel business centres (day-use). The Sheraton Grand and Novotel Da Nang both offer day-use rates for their business centres — useful for video calls requiring professional backgrounds or for meeting clients. Ask at reception; rates are typically $15–25/day including WiFi and printing.

Café Culture

Work Cafés

Da Nang has a café culture that functions for extended working sessions — strong WiFi, power sockets at most tables, staff who understand laptop workers, and a price point that makes a 4-hour session cost $3–6 in coffee.

What to look for. The best work cafés share common features: multiple power sockets per table, WiFi password displayed (rather than requested), natural light, and enough ambient noise to signal comfort with long stays. The An Thuong strip has over 30 cafés within a 500m radius — the ones with "WiFi: [speed]Mbps" signs outside are specifically signalling to remote workers.

Recommended work cafés in An Thuong. Cộng Cà Phê (communist-nostalgia aesthetic, coconut coffee, fast WiFi, always has tables available before 10am). Highlands Coffee (chain, consistent WiFi and air conditioning, outlets at every table, the most reliable choice for a 2-hour video call). La Cà (Vietnamese-owned specialty coffee, the most serious beans in the An Thuong area, quiet in the afternoons).

In Hải Châu. The cà phê trứng (egg coffee) culture in the old district is worth seeking out for the experience, but most traditional Vietnamese coffee shops have limited WiFi and no power sockets. For work sessions in Hải Châu, aim for any café with a visible WiFi sign and at least some Western-style seating rather than the low plastic chairs of a traditional street stall.

Beach cafés. Multiple beachside café-restaurants on My Khe operate WiFi good enough for email and light browsing, but unreliable for video calls (the upload speed on beach locations is typically 20–40 Mbps rather than the 100–200 Mbps available inland). Treat them as afternoon relaxation rather than primary working locations.

Connectivity

Internet, SIM Cards & Connectivity

Home / apartment internet. Viettel fibre is the dominant home internet provider in Da Nang — 100Mbps symmetrical is standard, 500Mbps plans available. Most landlords include internet in long-term rentals; if not, self-installation takes 2–3 business days and costs $8–12/month for the base 100Mbps plan. VNPT is an alternative with slightly broader residential coverage in older Hải Châu buildings.

SIM cards. Buy at Da Nang Airport arrivals or at any Viettel, Vietnamobile, or Vinaphone store in the city. Viettel has the best 4G/5G coverage throughout Da Nang and on the routes to Hoi An, the Marble Mountains, and Son Tra. A 30-day unlimited data plan: Viettel D60 — 60,000 VND ($2.40), 30GB at 4G/LTE then reduced speed. For heavy users, D120 (120,000 VND / $4.80) gives 60GB. eSIM is available from Viettel for compatible devices — register online before travel. You will need your passport to register a physical SIM in-store.

5G coverage. Da Nang was among Vietnam's first 5G rollout cities. 5G coverage is available in the city centre, An Thuong, and along the airport road. Son Tra Peninsula and some inland areas remain 4G only. For practical remote work purposes, 4G LTE is sufficient as a backup connection.

VPN. Some international services (Google Workspace features, certain streaming platforms) work inconsistently from Vietnamese IP addresses. A VPN subscription is worth having — NordVPN and ExpressVPN both have servers optimised for Vietnamese connections. Factor this into setup before arrival.

Cost of Living

Monthly Budget Breakdown

A full mì Quảng bowl at a MICHELIN Bib Gourmand restaurant costs $2.50 — the Da Nang food budget is genuinely low for the quality

Accommodation (largest variable). Studio apartment, An Thuong, no sea view, furnished: $250–350/month. Studio apartment, An Thuong, partial sea view: $350–500/month. One-bedroom apartment, Hải Châu district: $220–320/month. Short-term Airbnb (1–4 weeks): $35–70/night for comparable quality. Hotels: $45–80/night for a well-rated mid-range property on My Khe Beach.

Food. Street food and local restaurants: $8–15/day covers three full meals at the level of MICHELIN-listed local spots. Western-style café lunches (An Thuong): $6–10 per meal. Cooking at home (Bách Hoá Xanh supermarket): $100–150/month for groceries if cooking most dinners. Total food budget: $200–350/month depending on eating habits.

Transport. Grab for all daily transport: $50–80/month. Motorbike hire (full month): $150–200/month. Motorbike purchase (used Honda Wave): $400–600 one-time, resellable. For stays over two months, a used motorbike pays for itself in avoided Grab fares within 6–8 weeks.

Co-working or café working. Co-working membership: $80–130/month. Café-working budget (coffee + light meals): $60–100/month. If café-working exclusively: $60–100/month covers the coffee consumption for a full working day at most An Thuong establishments.

Total estimates by tier. Minimal (Hải Châu apartment, street food, café-work only): $600–750/month. Mid-range (An Thuong studio, co-working, mixed dining): $900–1,200/month. Comfortable (good apartment, full co-working membership, some restaurant dining, weekend trips): $1,300–1,600/month.

Visa Options

Vietnam Visa Guide

Vietnam's 90-day e-visa — available to 80+ nationalities — is the standard entry route for digital nomads planning 1–3 month stays

E-visa (90 days, single or multiple entry). Vietnam's e-visa — available to citizens of 80+ countries including the US, UK, EU, Australia, and Canada — allows stays of up to 90 days and is extendable once for a further 90 days. Apply at the official portal (evisa.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn) with a passport photo, a scanned passport, and a $25 fee. Processing time: 3 business days. This is the primary option for most digital nomads.

Visa exemption. Citizens of certain countries (Thailand, Singapore, Japan, South Korea, and several European nations) receive 15–45 day visa-free entry. Check current bilateral agreements — the exemption list expanded in 2023. Visa-exempt entry can sometimes be combined with a border run for a second entry period, but this is unreliable and not recommended for extended stays.

Five-year multiple-entry visa. Introduced in 2023 for nationals of countries with established bilateral agreements. Application via Vietnamese embassy or consulate; fees and processing times vary by country. This is the long-term option for established remote workers planning regular Vietnam stays.

Extension and border runs. The 90-day e-visa is extendable once at a Vietnamese immigration office ($25, straightforward process). After the maximum 180-day stay, a brief exit and re-entry (typically Laos or Cambodia by land, or a flight) resets the clock. In practice, most nomads doing a 3-month Da Nang stint use the e-visa without needing extensions.

Important: Vietnam visa rules change — always verify current requirements at your country's Vietnamese embassy website or the official evisa portal before booking flights. The above reflects conditions as of early 2026.

Seasonal Conditions

Working Through the Year

February – May (peak working season). The best combination of weather, productivity, and lifestyle: dry season beach access, temperatures in the 25–30°C range, low humidity, and the highest concentration of other long-term nomads in the city. March and April are optimal. Co-working spaces are busiest but not overcrowded. Accommodation prices at their highest for the year.

June – August (hot, good). Temperatures climb to 33–36°C and outdoor daytime activity decreases, but air-conditioned café and co-working culture is at its strongest. The beach is swimmable and evenings are pleasant. This is when the beach-cafe balance works best — morning swim, afternoon in a co-working space, evening back on the beach. Accommodation prices drop slightly from the March–April peak.

September – November (monsoon — manageable). The northeast monsoon brings October rainfall volumes of 400–600mm — significant, with occasional multi-day rain events. The beach lifestyle is largely suspended. Indoor working is unaffected; internet reliability is not impacted by rain. The upside: accommodation drops 25–40% in price, tourist numbers fall sharply, and the city is genuinely more liveable for day-to-day errands. Typhoon risk is real (Da Nang averages 1–2 direct typhoon impacts per decade) — check weather forecasts and have contingency plans.

December – January (shoulder season). Cooler (20–24°C), transitioning from monsoon. Beach swimming possible on good days. Christmas and New Year periods bring tourist price spikes for accommodation. The Tết holiday (late January / February) sees domestic tourism surge and many local services close for 1–2 weeks — plan around it or embrace it.
