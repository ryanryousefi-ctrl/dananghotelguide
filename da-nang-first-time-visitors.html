Da Nang is Vietnam's third-largest city and its most visited coastal destination ‚Äî a place where a clean, 20-kilometre beach sits 5km from an international airport, the food is excellent and cheap, and the day-trip options within 40km include a UNESCO World Heritage town (Hoi An), a mountain theme park with one of the world's most photographed bridges (Ba Na Hills), and a cluster of ancient Buddhist cave temples (Marble Mountains). Visas

Vietnam's visa-free policy covers most Western nationalities for 45 days ‚Äî UK, EU, Australia, New Zealand, Canada, USA, South Korea, Japan, and others. No prior application needed; you receive the stamp on arrival. If your nationality isn't on the list, or you need more than 45 days, the Vietnam e-visa (USD 25, valid 90 days, multiple entry) is applied for online at evisa.xuatnhapcanh.gov.vn before departure.

Important: Visa policy updates regularly. Always check the current list at the Vietnamese Ministry of Foreign Affairs before booking flights, not just at travel blogs ‚Äî including this one. The 45-day visa-free policy was expanded significantly in 2023 and further adjustments are possible.

Getting Around

Grab is the answer for most transport needs. The app works seamlessly in Da Nang, prices are fair, and drivers are used to picking up international tourists. GrabBike (motorbike) is faster and cheaper for solo travellers; GrabCar is better for groups or when carrying luggage. Download and set up before you land.

Rented motorbike (120,000‚Äì150,000 VND/day) is the best option for independent travellers comfortable with riding. Son Tra Peninsula in particular rewards self-navigation ‚Äî the summit road winding through the jungle with ocean views on both sides is one of the best rides in Vietnam and takes about 2 hours at a relaxed pace. A motorbike is also the practical way to reach the Marble Mountains early in the morning before tour groups arrive.

Bicycle works for the immediate My Khe Beach strip ‚Äî flat, 20km coastline road ‚Äî but isn't practical for city sightseeing or day trips further out.

Traffic note: Da Nang traffic is dense but manageable compared to Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City. The primary risk for tourists is underestimating Vietnamese traffic patterns ‚Äî priority is not automatic for pedestrians or at intersections. Cross streets by moving at a steady, predictable pace. Cars and motorbikes will flow around you if you're consistent.

Safety

Da Nang is one of the safest cities in Southeast Asia for tourists. Violent crime against foreigners is extremely rare. The issues that do occur are predictable and preventable:

1
Phone snatching ‚Äî Don't hold your phone visibly while standing on a road or seated on a motorbike. Grab-and-run by passing motorbikes occurs, primarily in busy areas. Keep your phone in your pocket when not actively using it.
2
Motorbike accidents ‚Äî By far the most common serious incident involving tourists. If you haven't ridden a motorbike recently, the combination of unfamiliar bike handling, Vietnamese traffic patterns, and unfamiliar roads is a real risk. Rent from a reputable shop, wear your helmet, start on quiet roads, and don't ride after drinking.
3
Taxi overcharging ‚Äî Fixed with Grab. Use it. If you use a metered taxi, stick to Mai Linh (green) or Vinasun (white) ‚Äî they're reliable. Refuse unlicensed taxis that approach you at the airport or tourist sites.
4
Sun and heat exhaustion ‚Äî June‚ÄìAugust temperatures of 35‚Äì38¬∞C combined with beach activity and tourism are a real health risk, particularly for visitors from cooler climates. Drink water constantly, use SPF 50, and plan strenuous outdoor activities (Ba Na Hills, Marble Mountains) in the early morning.
5
Rip currents at My Khe ‚Äî My Khe Beach has lifeguards and flag systems. Red flag means no swimming ‚Äî this is enforced during rough sea conditions, typically October‚ÄìFebruary. Flag warnings are genuine safety measures, not suggestions.

Common Questions

First-Time Visitors: FAQ

Is Da Nang safe for tourists?
Da Nang is one of the safest cities in Southeast Asia for tourists. Violent crime against foreigners is extremely rare. The most common issues are phone snatching from motorbikes and traffic accidents involving tourists on rented bikes. Standard precautions: don't hold your phone visibly on busy streets, wear a helmet, use Grab instead of unlicensed taxis, and swim only when the beach flag is green or yellow. The city has tourist police and a low baseline of crime by regional standards.
Do I need a visa for Vietnam?
Most Western nationalities receive 45 days visa-free ‚Äî UK, EU, Australia, New Zealand, Canada, USA, South Korea, Japan, and others. No prior application needed. If your nationality isn't covered, or you need more than 45 days, apply for the Vietnam e-visa online (USD 25, 90 days, 3 business days processing). Always verify current policy at the Vietnamese Ministry of Foreign Affairs website before booking ‚Äî the visa-free list updates periodically.
What is the best area to stay in Da Nang?
My Khe Beach is the best base for most first-time visitors ‚Äî direct beach access, the best concentration of hotels and restaurants, and easy Grab access to every attraction. An Thuong street (one block inland) is the sweet spot for walkable caf√©s and restaurants. The Han River city centre is good for those who prefer urban atmosphere over direct beach access and want lower hotel prices.
What is the best time to visit Da Nang?
March to May is ideal for first-timers: hot and sunny (28‚Äì33¬∞C), low-to-medium crowds, and hotel prices near their annual minimum. The beach season runs February to August. Avoid October and November ‚Äî Da Nang's rainy season brings heavy rain, cooler temperatures, and typhoon risk. December and January are dry and pleasant but hotel prices peak around the holiday period and Vietnamese New Year (T·∫øt).
How many days do you need in Da Nang?
Four to five days is the recommended minimum for a first visit that covers the main experiences without rushing ‚Äî Ba Na Hills, Marble Mountains, Hoi An day trip, Son Tra Peninsula, and real beach time. Three days is possible but tight. Two days means sacrificing either the beach or the day trips, which defeats the point of the destination.
Do I need to speak Vietnamese to get around Da Nang?
No ‚Äî English is widely spoken at tourist-facing businesses, hotels, and the main restaurant strips. Local restaurants, markets, and shops often have no English, but Google Translate's camera mode handles Vietnamese menus accurately and Grab handles transport without needing language. The gap between tourist Da Nang and local Da Nang is bridged almost entirely by these two apps. Learning a few basic phrases (xin ch√†o for hello, c·∫£m ∆°n for thank you) is appreciated but not necessary.
